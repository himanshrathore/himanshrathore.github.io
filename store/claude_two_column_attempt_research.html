Currently, I am investigating the two most massive satellite galaxies of our Milky Way --- the LMC & SMC (collectively known as the Clouds). I plan to use new astrometric data, high-resolution hydrodynamical simulations, and a variety of kinematic studies to probe their properties and their interaction history.


Distorted Dark Matter Halos of the Clouds


We show that the dark matter halos of the Clouds are significantly distorted, wherein the density field can deviate by ~60% relative to a spherical halo.

The mutual LMC-SMC interactions have significantly distorted their dark matter distributions. Modeling their complex dark matter distribution is important for accurately inferring the LMC-SMC interaction timescales, the timing of the recent close encounter and the SMC's dark matter content.

SMC - A Galaxy in Transformation


We showed that the SMC's gas is not rotating (contrary to the widely held belief), and that the gas velocity gradient is likely due to tidally driven outflows.

The SMC is in disequilibrium. This galaxy has an on-sky stellar extent of a few kpc, yet its line of sight depth is > 10 kpc, and the stellar & gas centers are separated by > 1 kpc. Using hydrodynamical simulations, we infer that the SMC's peculiar morphology and kinematics are consistent with a recent direct collision with the LMC.

The LMC's Disequilibrium Bar & The SMC's Dark Matter


We show that the LMC hosts a strong bar, consistent with the expectation of bar-galaxy co-evolution.

The LMC has a strange bar. The bar is offset from the disk center, tilted with the disk plane and does not drive gas inflows. In Rathore+2025(a), we study what drives the LMC's bar pattern speed and its pattern speed variation with radius.

Why does the LMC's bar show such strange properties despite being consistent with bar - galaxy co-evolution ?

The SMC's Dark Matter


The LMC bar's strange properties are explainable with a recent direct collision between the LMC & SMC. Excitingly, the bar's tilt is a direct probe of the SMC's dark matter content and distribution!

In Rathore+2025(b), we use hydrodynamical simulations to understand the response of the LMC's bar to a recent SMC collision. We find that the bar's unusual properties are a natural consequence of the tidal effects of the SMC's massive dark matter halo.

The LMC's Peculiar Globular Clusters


Using Gaia-DR3 astrometry, we identify kinematically peculiar Globular Clusters in the LMC. We hypothesize that these Clusters have been accreted from external galaxies.

LMC's Globular Clusters offer valuable insights into the LMC's accretion history and dark matter properties. I mentored two students - Tamojeet Roychowdhury and Navdha Sharma on this project, both of whom are now pursuing PhDs at UC Berkeley.

Star-forming S0 Galaxies


We find that star-forming S0 galaxies were likely quenched S0s in the past, whose star-formation has been rejuvenated possibly through minor mergers.

S0 galaxies possess well defined disks, but lack spiral arms and are generally expected to be quenched. Hence, the existence of star-forming S0s poses a challenge to galaxy evolution theories. In Rathore+2022, using data from the Hyper Suprime-Cam survey, we characterize the star-forming S0 population.

In addition to the Milky Way's neighbourhood, I have also performed investigations of more distant galaxies as well as the large scale behaviour of the Universe.


G-transition & the Hubble Tension


We find that a 4% larger value of the Gravitational Constant at lookback times > 70 Million years is preferred by the Cepheid-Supernova data.

The Hubble tension is a raging debate in cosmology about the current expansion rate of the universe, as quantified by the Hubble constant. Early universe probes like the Cosmic Microwave Background and Late universe probes like Supernovae give conflicting measurements. In Ruchika & Rathore, 2024, we explore an interesting possibility: a transition in the value of the gravitational constant.
